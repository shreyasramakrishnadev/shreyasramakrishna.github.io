Flights are booked — Boston to Anchorage and back. The rental car is
reserved, pickup and drop-off both at ANC. What's left is the part that's
actually hard: building out 11 days of itinerary and lodging, with an
8.5-year-old and a 2.5-year-old along for all of it.

Somewhere in the middle of a spreadsheet a few nights ago, I realized I
was doing this trip the way I'd run a POV — mapping dependencies before
committing to anything, and risk-mapping what could derail a day before
it happens, instead of just listing places we'd like to see.

## The real constraints

A wish list of glaciers and hikes isn't a plan. What actually shapes the
plan:

- **Two very different kids.** An 8.5-year-old who can handle a full day
  of activity but has a limit on back-to-back "boring" driving days, and
  a 2.5-year-old whose entire day can be derailed by a missed nap. These
  aren't soft preferences — they're closer to hard requirements the
  whole itinerary gets built around.
- **Weather.** A lot of what makes Alaska worth visiting is
  weather-dependent — glacier viewing, certain hikes, wildlife spotting.
  Every day built around one of these needs a fallback, the same way a
  POV needs a plan B when a customer's environment doesn't cooperate.
- **Food.** My wife is vegetarian, which means meals aren't a
  detail to figure out on the fly — it's its own planning thread running
  alongside lodging and activities, especially in smaller towns where
  options might be limited.
- **Pacing.** Don't stack busy days back to back. We're deliberately
  interspersing full, active days with easier, low-key ones — partly for
  the kids to recover from long drives, partly because a trip that's
  "go go go" for 11 straight days isn't actually fun for anyone. It's
  the same instinct as slowing down mid-POV to make sure everyone's
  actually aligned before pushing forward — a pause now avoids a bigger
  problem three days later.

## The itinerary

Eleven days, mostly a loop with a couple of longer legs:

![Alaska trip itinerary route: Anchorage to Girdwood to Seward to Talkeetna to Denali to Fairbanks to Chena Hot Springs and back to Anchorage](/images/Alaska_Itinerary.png)

The busy/easy interspersing mostly took care of itself once the drive
times were on the table. Seward gets three nights — the longest single
stay on the trip, deliberately, so we're not packing and unpacking every
night right out of the gate. Denali is the other anchor stop, and the
one where weather-dependent planning matters most. Chena Hot Springs is
the easiest day on paper: one short drive, one clear destination, built
in right after a longer stretch. And the return leg — 6.5 hours,
Fairbanks straight back to Anchorage, no stops — is the one place with
zero slack, which makes it the leg most likely to test the risk-mapping
instinct from earlier.

That pattern wasn't fully intentional going in; it's closer to what the
dependencies forced once we respected the driving-with-kids limit. What's
still being worked out inside this frame: exact activities per stop, and
the vegetarian food situation in the smaller towns — Seward, Talkeetna,
and Chena are the three where that's going to take the most advance
planning, versus Anchorage and Fairbanks where options should be easier
to find on the fly.

One decision worth calling out for anyone planning something similar:
you could fly out of Fairbanks instead of driving the 6.5 hours back to
Anchorage. We looked at it and went with the drive — return flights out
of Anchorage were cheaper than out of Fairbanks, and a lot of rental car
companies charge a one-way drop-off premium if you return the car
somewhere other than where you picked it up. The math didn't favor
splitting the trip that way once we added it up, but it's a real option
if your flight pricing or rental terms land differently.

## Where things stand

Flights and car are done. The route above is close to final, activities
still getting filled in. I'll write the second half of this once we're
back — what the plan looked like on paper versus what 11 days in Alaska
with two kids actually turned into. My guess going in: the plan survives
contact with day one, and not much past it.
