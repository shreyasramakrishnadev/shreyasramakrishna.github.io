A lot of SE training focuses on the deal in front of you - the demo, the
POV, the technical objection in the room. Less gets said about the thing
that determines whether any of that survives after you leave the room:
whether there's someone on the customer's side who'll keep advocating for
you when you're not there.

That's a technical champion. And building one isn't a side effect of
doing good technical work — it's a distinct skill, and arguably one of
the highest-leverage things an SE does across a deal's lifecycle.

## Why this is an SE responsibility, not just an AE one

Champions get talked about constantly in sales methodology — MEDDIC,
MEDDPICC, every framework has a "C" for champion. But champion-building
often gets treated as the AE's job, with the SE showing up to support
the technical conversation. That's backwards, or at least incomplete.

The AE builds the business relationship. The SE builds technical
credibility — and technical credibility is exactly what a technical
champion needs from *you* in order to have credibility internally,
themselves. If the champion doesn't trust your technical judgment, they
can't stake their own reputation on recommending you. That trust gets
built in the technical conversations — which is your seat, not the AE's.

## Identifying the right person, early

This is the part that's easiest to get wrong: mistaking engagement for
championship potential. Someone who asks a lot of questions in a demo
might just be curious, or skeptical, or doing due diligence for someone
else entirely. A real champion candidate usually shows a different
signal: they start talking about how *they'd* use this, not just whether
it works. They ask about rollout, about their team's workflow, about
what internal conversations they'd need to have to get this approved.
That's someone already doing the internal selling in their head.

The mistake is waiting until late in a deal to figure out who your
champion is. By then, you've usually already spent your best technical
credibility-building moments on the wrong audience. Identifying
champion-potential early means you can deliberately invest technical
time with that person specifically — not just present to whoever's in
the room.

## The part that's genuinely underrated: don't rely on just one

Here's the pattern that shows up across a lot of stalled or dead deals,
and it's rarely about the product: the deal had exactly one champion,
and something happened to that person. They changed roles. They left the
company. They got pulled onto a different priority. And the deal, which
looked healthy a month earlier, quietly stalled — because all of the
internal advocacy was sitting with one individual who was no longer
positioned (or present) to provide it.

Multi-threading a champion relationship isn't just a nice-to-have risk
mitigation — it's closer to a hard requirement for a deal that's supposed
to survive contact with reality. That means deliberately building
technical credibility with more than one person on the customer's
technical side, even when one person is clearly your strongest advocate.
It's slower. It takes more of your time to build two or three real
relationships instead of one. But a single point of failure in your
champion strategy is exactly that — a single point of failure — and it's
one of the more preventable ways a good deal quietly falls apart.

## Why this is worth treating as a discipline

None of this is exotic. It's closer to just taking seriously something
that's easy to let happen passively instead of deliberately. Champions
who happen organically are a nice outcome. Champions who are
deliberately identified early and multi-threaded are a strategy — and
the difference between the two tends to show up exactly when you're not
in the room to see it.
