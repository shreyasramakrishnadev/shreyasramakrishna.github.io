I left India for Boston in December 2011, for a Master's Degree in Computer
Science at Northeastern University. I'd grown up there, worked there for two years
before that — it wasn't foreign to me. This year, we went back for my
brother-in-law's wedding in December. We expected to be there a few
weeks. We ended up staying six months.

## A wedding, then a waiting game

Our visa appointments were originally scheduled for December — timed
around the trip, so we'd wrap up both at once. Then, on December 3rd, the
State Department announced an expansion of social media vetting
procedures for H-1B and H-4 visa applicants. Appointments across the
board got pushed back as consulates adjusted to the new process — ours
moved all the way to June. We eventually managed to get them moved up
again, to early April, once additional slots opened. So a short trip home
for a family wedding turned into an extended, mostly uncertain stretch of
waiting to find out exactly when we'd actually be able to come back.

I'm not interested in relitigating the policy itself here. It was a
change that happened while we were already mid-trip, and there wasn't
much to do but adjust and wait it out. What I didn't expect was how much
room that waiting would eventually leave for everything else.

## A city that had moved on without me

Bangalore had changed more than I expected. I'd last been back three
years earlier, and even in that short span, the traffic felt like it had
roughly doubled. It was a small, constant reminder of how much keeps
moving in a place even after you've built a life somewhere else — a
little disorienting, honestly, in a way I hadn't prepared for.

What hadn't changed was how easy it was to fall back into old
friendships. I reconnected with people I'd known since elementary school
— kept in touch with loosely for fifteen years, but hadn't sat across
from in just as long. Six months turned out to be long enough to build
something new, too, not just revisit something old. There's a particular
kind of relief in realizing a friendship can pick back up exactly where
it left off, no matter how much the city around it has changed.

## What the six months gave the kids

My son turned 2 while we were there, and he loved it — genuinely,
unreservedly. Grandparents, extended family, a house full of people who
doted on him constantly. He went to a local daycare for a couple of
months, which gave him routine in between all the family time. Watching
him form that kind of easy, everyday bond with his grandparents — not a
once-a-year video call, but real familiarity — was one of the clearest
"this is worth it" moments of the whole trip.

My daughter turned 8 during the trip, and her experience was the most complicated at first. 
She missed second grade back home. She missed second grade back in the States, so we set
her up with a private tutor for online classes to stay current with her
actual curriculum — which worked academically, but wasn't the same as a
classroom. She missed her friends, and she missed recess — the specific,
ordinary thing of running around a schoolyard with kids her age. That
loss was real, and I don't want to smooth over it.

But what she found instead surprised all of us. My mom made it something
of a personal mission to teach her Kannada — before the trip, she could
only understand a little of it. Two months later, she was actually
speaking it. That's remarkable on both ends: my mom's patience, and my
daughter's willingness to just absorb a language by living inside it. She
made new friends with the neighbors' kids, and they'd go out and play
most evenings — the yard, the parking lot, the quiet street my parents
live on. Not a schoolyard, but its own kind of entertaining to her, in a
way I don't think she expected either. She came home having lost some
things and gained one she couldn't have gained any other way.

## The people who made it feel easy

If the six months felt manageable rather than like an imposition, that wasn't luck. 
It was my mom, my dad, my two aunts, and my sister, who all showed up for us every 
single day — not just logistically, but with a kind of steady
emotional presence that made a long, uncertain stretch feel held instead
of heavy. My mom's patience teaching my daughter Kannada was one visible
piece of it, but it was really the whole household — my dad included —
absorbing four extra people into their daily rhythm without ever once
making it feel like a burden. My wife got real, unhurried time with her
own parents too, and with her brother's new wife, who'd just joined the
family after the wedding. None of that happens in the usual once-a-year,
two-week visit. I don't think I fully appreciated, until this trip, how
much of a soft landing that kind of family support actually is.

## Places I hadn't seen in years — and some I'd never seen at all

I made a trip out to my father's ancestral home, where my oldest aunt and
her son still live. I hadn't been back in fifteen years, and there was
something grounding about walking through it again after that long —
less nostalgia, more like touching a fixed point while everything else
about the trip felt uncertain.

We did some real traveling as a family too. We saw the Adiyogi statue —
over 40 feet tall, genuinely striking in person. We spent a weekend in
Dandeli with my sister's family and her kids, white-water rafting and
filling the days with the kind of chaos that's much better with cousins
along for it. And for the first time in my life, I saw the Taj Mahal, and
I visited Vrindavan, where Krishna is said to have grown up. Both had
been on some mental list for years without ever quite happening — waiting
turned out to be exactly what finally made room for them.

## The food, everywhere

No part of this trip happened without incredible food, and it's worth
its own mention rather than a footnote. Home-cooked meals my mom made
without a second thought, the spread at my in-laws' whenever we visited,
restaurant meals that reminded me exactly why six months isn't nearly
enough time to eat through everything a city like Bangalore has to
offer. It's a small thing to put next to visas and wedding photos and
statues, but it wasn't small at all while we were there — some of the
best conversations of the whole trip happened over a meal, not despite
one.

## What I'd tell someone in the same spot

The uncertainty around the appointments never fully went away. It sat in
the back of our minds the entire stretch — not loud, not consuming, but
always there. We tried to stay in the present as much as we could. Make
lemonade with the lemons we'd been dealt, as the saying goes — and most
days, thanks in large part to the people around us, we managed it.

Back in Boston now, back to the usual rhythm. I don't think about that
stretch as "the trip that went wrong." It started as a wedding, stayed
longer than we expected, and — because of the people who made it easy —
gave us far more than we walked in expecting.
