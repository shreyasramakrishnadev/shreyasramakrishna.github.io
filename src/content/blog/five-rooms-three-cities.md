I flew back to India from Boston for a few weeks this year, and I'm still
processing everything that came out of it. Five sessions, three cities,
roughly 600 people — and one thread running through all of it: people
weren't looking for answers about AI. They were looking for someone to
think alongside.

## Bengaluru: the room that didn't want to leave

![Speaking at the Datadog Summit Bengaluru](/images/DD_Workshop_BLR.jpeg)

*At the Datadog Summit in Bengaluru, I spent four hours with 120+ engineers
and technology leaders on GenAI and LLM monitoring. It wasn't a lecture —
more a working session, hands on keyboards, real problems on the table.*

The part that stayed with me happened after the session ended. People
lingered. They wanted to think through their own AI architectures out
loud, right there in the room. Those hallway conversations were the whole
point of flying halfway around the world.

## On stage at AWS Summit, Bengaluru

![Speaking at the AWS Summit Bengaluru](/images/AWSSummit_BLR.jpeg)
![Speaking at the AWS Summit Bengaluru](/images/AWS_Speaker_BLR.jpeg)
![Speaking at the AWS Summit Bengaluru](/images/SR_Speaker.jpeg)

*A different kind of room: 300+ people, C-suite down to platform
engineers, and me on stage talking about monitoring GenAI applications —
going beyond the traditional golden signals.*

Afterward, people tracked
down our booth. Not for a pitch. Just to keep talking. That kind of energy
isn't something you can manufacture with slides.

## Kochi: an hour that maybe shifted something

![Speaking at the AWS Summit Bengaluru](/images/SR_Booth_AWS_BLR.jpeg)

*In Kochi, hosted by the ITLC Kochi community, I spent a full hour with
100+ IT leaders from across South India. We started with observability
and found our way to GenAI. For a lot of people in that room, it was
their first real introduction to Datadog.*

Walking out of that one, knowing you might have shifted how a group of
leaders thinks about something — that's a feeling that's hard to put into
words, and I'm not going to try much harder than that here.

## The two quieter rooms

Not every moment on this trip was a stage or a packed hall. Two of the
ones that mattered most were much smaller.

## In Delhi.. 

I pulled up a chair with our Enterprise Sales team. No slides —
just a real conversation about GenAI fundamentals, and how to walk into a
C-suite discovery call with actual confidence instead of a script.

## And, In Bengaluru..
A different table, a different team — Mid-Market Sales this
time — for a candid session on competitive positioning. How to read a
deal. Who to actually talk to. When to walk away.

Both of those conversations kept going long after the session was
supposed to end. That's usually the sign you hit on something real.

## What I keep coming back to

GenAI is moving fast, and the teams shipping it need to see inside what
they've built — traces, cost, latency, model behavior, all of it. That's
the exact problem I get to work on at Datadog, and this trip was a
reminder of why I like being in this space.

Grateful for every one of those hours. Now — back to Boston, and back to
work.
